~A Fantastic Submarine Introduction: A Journey Ahead of Its Time When 20,000 Leagues Under the Sea was published in 1870, readers were shocked and fascinated. Jules Verne did not write only an adventure story. He imagined a future where humans could travel deep beneath the ocean, explore unknown worlds, and live for long periods underwater. At that time, the sea was still a mystery, and much of it had never been explored. Verne turned this mystery into a dramatic and believable journey. The novel is now considered one of the earliest and most important science fiction stories. What makes it special is how closely it connects imagination with real science. Verne carefully studied the technology of his time and then pushed it further. His famous submarine, the Nautilus, feels powerful and realistic because it is based on ideas that already existed, even if they were still experimental. One real example is the USS Alligator, built in 1862. It was the first submarine used by the United States Navy. Designed by Brutus de Villeroi, it was created to fight enemy ironclad ships during the American Civil War. The Alligator was 47 feet long and included surprising features, such as an air-purifying system and a chamber that allowed divers to leave the submarine underwater. At first, it even used hand-powered oars to move. Although the Alligator was small and limited compared to the Nautilus, the connection is clear. Both represent a bold idea: that humans could control the deep sea instead of fearing it. Verne transformed early submarine science into a dramatic vision of freedom, power, and danger beneath the waves. This balance of science and drama is why the novel still feels alive today.~ Quiz ?Why did 20,000 Leagues Under the Sea surprise readers when it was published? |It imagined realistic underwater travel before it was common| It described life on other planets It focused only on history It avoided scientific ideas ?Inference: Why was the ocean mysterious in the 1800s? |Much of it was unexplored and unseen| People were forbidden to sail Technology made it boring It was already well known ?What makes the Nautilus feel believable to readers?
