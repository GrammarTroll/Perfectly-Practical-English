One morning, Little Red Riding Hood’s mother said, “Your grandmother is ill and weak. Take this basket of bread and butter to her. Walk straight through the forest and do not leave the path. Do not speak to strangers.” Little Red Riding Hood promised to obey. She took the basket and began her walk through the forest. The forest was quiet and beautiful. Birds sang in the trees, and sunlight passed gently through the leaves. Little Red Riding Hood walked happily, thinking only of her grandmother. Soon, she met a wolf. He was large, with sharp eyes and a smooth voice. “Good morning, little girl,” said the wolf politely. “Good morning,” she answered, forgetting her mother’s warning. “Where are you going so early?” asked the wolf. “I am going to my grandmother’s house,” said Little Red Riding Hood. “She is ill, and I am bringing her food.” The wolf smiled to himself. “Where does your grandmother live?” he asked. “She lives at the edge of the forest,” said the girl. “Under the three large oak trees.” The wolf thought quickly. He wanted to eat both the girl and the grandmother, but he needed to be clever. “Look at the beautiful flowers,” he said. “Why don’t you pick some for your grandmother?” Little Red Riding Hood looked around. She saw many flowers and thought they would make her grandmother happy. “That is a good idea,” she said. She left the path and began picking flowers. Meanwhile, the wolf ran quickly through the forest to the grandmother’s house. He knocked on the door. “Who is there?” asked the grandmother weakly. “It is Little Red Riding Hood,” said the wolf, pretending to sound sweet. “I have brought you food.” “Pull the latch and come in,” said the grandmother. The wolf entered and swallowed the grandmother whole. He then put on her clothes, lay down in her bed, and pulled the blankets up to his chin. After some time, Little Red Riding Hood reached the house. She knocked on the door. “Come in,” said the wolf, in a low voice. He entered the house and saw the wolf lying in the bed. “So here you are,” said the hunter. “I have been looking for you.” The hunter noticed that the wolf’s stomach was moving. He took his knife and carefully cut it open. Out came Little Red Riding Hood, alive but frightened. Then out came the grandmother, weak but unharmed. They were grateful and joyful. The hunter filled the wolf’s stomach with heavy stones and sewed it shut. When the wolf woke and tried to run, he fell down and died from the weight. The boy thanked him and went back to the inn. That night, when the innkeeper tried to steal the stick, the boy cried, “Stick, beat!” The stick flew into the air and began to strike the innkeeper. “Stop! Stop!” the innkeeper cried. “I will return everything!” “Not until you do,” said the boy. The innkeeper returned the magic tablecloth and the magic goat. Then the boy said, “Stick, stop!” The boy went home with all three gifts. He and his mother were never poor again. They always had food, money, and safety.
