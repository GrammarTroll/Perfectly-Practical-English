~Concise Games These games represent the most common activity types from ESL websites. Only the gist is here, consolidated with the best elements of similar activities and suggested variations.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Rock Scissors Paper Rock, scissors, paper can be played the traditional way, and it can be used to decide who goes first in any other game. Students use their hands to make rock, scissors, or paper. Rock breaks scissors, scissors cuts paper, paper covers rock. Flash cards with different foods, animals, characters, or things on them can be used in a similar way for students to trump each others' cards. Or give each student three cards. Then students find partners and play rock, scissors, paper. The loser chooses one of their cards. The winner asks three questions to guess what is on the card. If he guesses correctly, he keeps the card. The student who collects the most cards wins.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Tic-Tac-Toe Write categories in the squares, for example animals, celebrities or medical ailments. Have students answer a question before they can mark the square. Or put words in the boxes and have the students make a sentence with that word. If the sentence is correct, students can place an X or O in the square. Try using wet tissue paper, to throw at the board to select squares.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Scattergories (Word Association) Divide the class into three teams. Offer 10 categories. Pick a category and pick a letter. The students have two minutes to come up with words in that category that start with that letter. Teams get a point for each word they come up with, but they cannot repeat themselves, and if two teams write down the same word, nobody gets a point. The students with the longest list win that round.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Hangman Hangman is usually just a game where students guess the letters in a word. Each time they make a mistake, another part of the stick man is drawn under a hangman's noose. If the figure is completed before the students guess the word, they lose. The student who guesses the final letter or guess is the word wins the round. Other variations of hangman include: Hang Hello Kitty, Shark, Hangman Corrections, Definition Hangman, Occupations Hangman. Hangman can also be played to elicit definitions, to make corrections, or create word lists. The teacher can give clues such as what part of speech a word is, or what tense it is in.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Password and Charades Either single words or entire sentences are written on pieces of paper. The students here are, to say the very least, tough customers when it comes to getting them to read, write, listen or speak, much less tolerate something as utterly boring as the mundane grammar fare that our rigorous syllabus requires. Not only that, but their English is not real good. Since I am new to ESL, I was very pleased with myself, when one day, when I felt like all hope was lost and I was about to pull my hair out, I spontaneously invented a game that solved all my problems, and I hope it can solve yours too since a good English teacher is always thinking up new games to play. I think my humble idea is worthy to deserve submitting to this web site where I often resort for fresh ideas to use with my classes. Perhaps by submitting this game, I can repay, in a small way, the many good ideas I have found here from time to time. Here is how I came up with my idea. It was right before a particularly difficult class; you know...great kids, and very bright too, but not very interested in English, and a bit shy to speak out in class. I suddenly realized I had nothing prepared! The students were already coming in, and while I was taking roll, the idea hit me like a thunderbolt. How to play: It is a variation of Hangman, except that you play it with complete paragraphs. You do not need any supplies except for a whiteboard and a whiteboard marker, and a whiteboard eraser. It works equally well with large or small classes, but it is even more fun with just a few students. Unfortunately my class size is usually about 40, so we normally have to play two rounds so everyone has a chance to have a turn. Step 1: I usually have my students sit down, although you can have them stand up or move around to get their blood pumping. Step 2: I tell the students I am thinking of a paragraph. I do not tell them what the paragraph is, because that would spoil the game. I avoid making facial expressions or making eye contact, or focusing my attention on any classroom objects, or anything that might give away part of the paragraph. Step 3: Then the students take turns calling out alphabet letters until the entire paragraph emerges. Step 4: Then I write in the letters of the paragraph on the whiteboard. This is where a whiteboard marker comes in handy. You can call the students up one at a time to write the letters if you need to extend the activity. You can also extend the activity by writing a really long paragraph. This will create more of a challenge for brighter classes. Step 5: Declare the winner! The student who completes the paragraph is the winner. Then the winner leads the class in reading the paragraph aloud, so the students not only get spelling, reading, listening and speaking practice in the same lesson, but they also sharpen their pronunciation skills. Variation: To spice it up, if a student calls out a letter that has already been called previously, I erase that letter. This is where a whiteboard eraser comes in handy. This really keeps students on their toes because once the letter is erased, it is fair game for the next student to call out that letter again and get it back on the board. Of course this game gets pretty competitive because I always give my students a reward like a no-homework-card, or a get-to-leave-class-early-card, or a get-out-of-class-free-card. My students love the game and would rather do that than anything else I have ever tried before. It has become an overnight ESL sensation at my school where I am the only foreign English speaker, and we do not have many resources or use a textbook. If you try it in your class, please email me and let me know how it goes and what you think of it! I hope my instructions were clear! If you have any suggestions or ideas to make my game better, I would be glad to hear from you. I think this game is adaptable for nearly any subject you teach; not just English! Caution: This game can get very noisy indeed while students wait for their turn to call out an alphabet letter. It is best to have worksheets handy! A good teacher is always prepared. Remember: Even a good lesson bombs sometimes, or doesn’t go as planned, so never give up. You can also bribe students to play the game with small hard candies or sticks of gum as treats, but make sure the students put the wrappers in the bin. There, I think that about covers it! Each match represents a clash at the front line, where careful attacks, smart blocking, and timing decide the outcome. The game is designed to be: Quick – playable in 5–15 minutes Simple – easy to learn with minimal rules Flexible – playable with as few as 4 cards, or expanded to 9 or more Players take turns attacking with their Guardians while defending with limited resources. Every decision matters, because once a Guardian is discarded, it’s gone for the rest of the game. The goal is straightforward: deal 3 damage to your opponent before they do the same to you.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Making Guardians Cards Each player designs their own Guardians before playing. All cards are called Guardians, but each one has a specific role. You may play using: Quick Play: exactly 4 Guardians Standard Play: 9 or more Guardians The required information for each Guardian type is listed below. 1. Frontline Guardian (Main Guardian) Every player must have exactly one Frontline Guardian. This Guardian represents your leader and is your most important card. Required Information Guardian Name Type: Frontline Guardian Power (recommended: 3) Effect (optional, very simple) Effect Guidelines (Optional) If you include an effect, it should be easy to remember, such as: “Once per game, this attack deals +1 damage.” “When this Guardian attacks, draw 1 card.” The Frontline Guardian should usually be the strongest Guardian in your deck. 2. Support Guardians Support Guardians assist your Frontline Guardian by attacking or blocking. Required Information Guardian Name Type: Support Guardian Power (recommended: 1–2) Effect (optional) Rules for Support Guardians Only 1 Support Guardian may be in the Support Zone at a time. A Support Guardian may: Attack, or Be discarded to block an attack. Example Simple Effects (Optional) “When this Guardian blocks, draw 1 card.” “When this Guardian attacks, it gets +1 damage this turn.” 3. Shield Guardian Each player must have at least one Shield Guardian. This represents your strongest defensive option. Required Information Guardian Name Type: Shield Guardian Fixed Effect Discard this Guardian to block one attack completely.
